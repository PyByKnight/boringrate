§ Coverage primer

Every coverage type, actually explained.

Liability

Required in most states

Liability pays for other people's injuries and property damage when you cause an accident. It does not cover your car or your own injuries.

It's expressed as three numbers. 100/300/100 means: up to $100k per injured person, $300k per accident total, and $100k for property damage. State minimums are typically far lower — often 25/50/25 or less.

Why state minimums are often not enough: A serious accident with injuries can easily exceed $100k in medical bills alone. If damages exceed your coverage limit, you pay the difference personally. Your net worth is what's at risk.

Rule of thumb: buy at least as much liability as your net worth. If you have significant assets, a $1–2M personal umbrella policy stacked on top of 250/500/250 limits is often the most cost-efficient way to protect them — often $200–400/year on its own.

Collision

Required if you have a loan or lease

Collision pays to repair or replace your car when you hit something — another vehicle, a guardrail, a pothole. It applies regardless of fault.

Whether it's worth buying depends on your car's value. The classic test: if your annual collision premium exceeds 10% of your car's current market value, you may be over-insuring. On a $4,000 car paying $500/year for collision, you'd need to file a claim every 8 years just to break even — and deductibles eat further into the payout.

If you have a loan or lease: your lender requires collision. You don't have the option to drop it until you own the car outright.

When you do carry collision, a higher deductible ($1,000 instead of $500) meaningfully reduces your premium. If you have the savings to cover the deductible, it's almost always the right call.

Comprehensive

Required if you have a loan or lease

Comprehensive covers damage that isn't from a collision: theft, fire, flooding, hail, falling trees, hitting an animal. It's the "everything else" policy.

Comprehensive is typically cheap relative to its benefit — often $100–200/year for most cars. The bar for skipping it is much higher than for collision. The main reason not to carry it: your car is worth so little that even a total loss payout wouldn't justify years of premiums.

If you're on the fence: get the comprehensive-only premium from your insurer. If it's under $150/year on a car worth $5k+, keeping it is almost always the mathematically correct decision.

Uninsured / Underinsured Motorist

Required in some states

UM/UIM covers your injuries and vehicle damage when the at-fault driver has no insurance, or not enough to cover your losses.

About 1 in 8 U.S. drivers is uninsured. In some states, it's closer to 1 in 4. If an uninsured driver totals your car and sends you to the hospital, without UM/UIM you're suing someone who has nothing — and absorbing the loss yourself.

This is one of the most underrated coverages available. It's typically cheap ($50–150/year) and fills a gap that your own liability policy does nothing about. Buy it at limits matching your liability coverage.

Medical Payments / PIP

Required in no-fault states

MedPay and Personal Injury Protection (PIP) cover your own medical bills after an accident, regardless of who was at fault. PIP also covers lost wages and sometimes childcare in certain states.

If you have comprehensive health insurance, MedPay is largely redundant — your health plan already covers the same bills. The exception: MedPay has no deductible or co-pay, so on a high-deductible health plan it can still save you money on a small claim.

If you're uninsured or underinsured on health: MedPay is not optional. An ER visit following an accident can run $20–50k. Without health insurance or PIP, you're paying that out-of-pocket.

If you live in a no-fault state (Florida, Michigan, New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and several others), PIP is required by law and will be included in your policy automatically.

Gap Insurance

Financed and leased vehicles only

Gap insurance pays the difference between what your insurer pays out on a totaled car and what you still owe on the loan or lease.

New cars depreciate fast — often 15–25% in the first year. If you financed with a small down payment and your car is totaled in year one or two, you can easily owe $5–10k more than the car is currently worth. Gap covers that shortfall so you're not paying off a loan on a car you no longer own.

When gap makes sense: you financed more than 80% of the purchase price, the car is under 3 years old, or you have a long loan term (72+ months). When it doesn't: large down payment, car holds its value well (trucks, some SUVs), or you've paid off most of the loan.

Dealers routinely pitch gap at the point of sale. It's almost always cheaper through your auto insurer ($20–40/year) than the dealer's version ($500–700 rolled into the loan at interest).

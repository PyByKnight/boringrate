Florida auto insurance rates are dropping. Here's what the filings actually say.

State Farm, Progressive, GEICO, USAA, and Allstate have all filed rate decreases for Florida drivers in 2026. If you haven't shopped recently, your renewal is probably higher than it needs to be.

BoringRate Editorial  ·  May 2026
Boring Research Alert

Florida's top 5 auto carriers are averaging an 8% rate decrease for 2026, covering 78% of the state's drivers. This is the most significant rate relief Florida has seen in over a decade.

For years, Florida drivers absorbed some of the most aggressive rate increases in the country. A combination of litigation abuse, assignment-of-benefits fraud, hurricane losses, and skyrocketing reinsurance costs made Florida one of the most expensive auto insurance markets in the US. That dynamic has reversed.

The driver is tort reform. Legislation passed in 2022 and 2023 has sharply reduced insurance litigation in the state — filings have fallen more than 35% since 2021. That reduction in legal costs is now flowing directly into carrier rate filings, and the Florida Office of Insurance Regulation has been approving decreases consistently.

What the filings show

These aren't estimates or projections. These are approved carrier filings with the Florida OIR:

Carrier Filed decrease Notes
State Farm −10.1% Multiple approved decreases since 2024
AAA −15.0% Three separate decreases over past year
Progressive −8.0% Plus separate $1B refund to policyholders
USAA −7.0% Effective May 2026, military members only
Allstate −7.0% Applies to over 171,000 Florida drivers
GEICO Decrease Rate relief for 700,000+ customers, April 2026

One carrier outside the top five has indicated a decrease of 16.5% — the largest single filing in the current cycle. In total, 42 companies covering personal auto lines have filed for rate decreases in the past year.

Why your renewal might not reflect this yet

Rate decreases apply at renewal, not mid-term. If your policy renewed before the decrease took effect at your carrier, you're still on the old rate. You won't see the adjustment until your next renewal — unless you shop and switch to a carrier whose decrease is already in effect.

This is the part aggregators won't tell you: your current carrier's renewal notice reflects their rates and your current coverage. It does not tell you what other carriers would charge for equivalent coverage. The only way to know whether you're paying more than you need to is to get at least two or three competing quotes.

Florida's loss ratio context: In 2025, Florida ranked first in the nation for the lowest personal auto liability loss ratio at 52.5% — the lowest recorded in 15 years. Carriers are profitable in Florida right now, which is exactly what enables and incentivizes rate reductions. This isn't charity — it's competition for a market that's become attractive again.

What's still uncertain

The rate environment is favorable, but a few things are worth watching. Vehicle repair costs continue to rise as cars become more technologically complex — sensors, cameras, and driver-assistance systems embedded in bumpers and mirrors cost multiples of what a simple replacement used to. Whether the litigation savings permanently outpace repair inflation is the key question for the medium term.

Additionally, not every carrier has filed decreases. Smaller carriers outside the top five may lag the market or hold rates flat. Shopping only your current carrier's renewal misses this entirely.

If you're a Florida driver and your policy renews in the next 90 days, this is the moment to spend 30 minutes getting quotes. The market has moved in your favor — but only if you look.

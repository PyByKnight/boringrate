OK · Above Average

Oklahoma rates are rising — tornado alley isn't cheap to insure in.

At $2,648 per year, Oklahoma drivers pay above the national average. Severe weather, a high uninsured motorist rate, and increasing litigation costs are the primary drivers. Shopping aggressively is your best defense.

BoringRate Editorial  ·  May 2026
TLDR
  • Oklahoma averages around $2,648/year — above average, driven by tornado and severe weather exposure
  • Rates rose +27% since 2023 — Tornado Alley is getting more expensive to insure
  • Comprehensive coverage is critical in Oklahoma — hail, wind, and tornado damage occur more frequently here than most states
  • State Farm and USAA rank best; Shelter Insurance is a strong Midwestern regional option here
  • Auto-Owners is worth quoting for homeowners looking to bundle — bundling discounts are among the largest available in OK

Oklahoma sits above the national average for auto insurance at $2,648 per year for full coverage. The state's location in the heart of tornado alley means severe weather risk — hail, high winds, and occasional tornadoes — adds significantly to comprehensive claims. Carriers price this statewide, which means even drivers in low-traffic rural areas pay for the weather risk of the whole state.

Oklahoma also has one of the highest uninsured motorist rates in the country, estimated at over 13% of drivers. That risk gets distributed across all insured drivers in the form of higher uninsured motorist premiums. The Oklahoma City and Tulsa metros drive the highest rates in the state due to traffic density, accident frequency, and above-average theft rates.

What Oklahoma drivers can do

The carrier spread in Oklahoma can be significant — well over $700 per year between the cheapest and most expensive option for equivalent coverage. State Farm, GEICO, and Progressive all compete aggressively in Oklahoma, and shopping at renewal is consistently the most effective savings strategy. Bundling home and auto can also produce meaningful discounts in this market.

Oklahoma average: $2,648/year full coverage. National average: ~$2,500. Oklahoma drivers pay roughly 6% above the national average — weather risk and uninsured motorist exposure are the primary culprits.

Frequently asked questions

What is the cheapest car insurance in Oklahoma?
State Farm and GEICO are typically the most competitive national carriers in Oklahoma. Oklahoma Farm Bureau is a member-based option that can price below national carriers for eligible drivers. USAA is the best option for military and veterans. Oklahoma rates have risen significantly due to severe weather — shopping annually is important to avoid rate drift. Enter your ZIP to compare.

What are Oklahoma's minimum auto insurance requirements?
Oklahoma requires 25/50/25 liability coverage — $25,000 per person / $50,000 per accident bodily injury and $25,000 property damage. Oklahoma is a tort state with no mandatory PIP. Uninsured motorist coverage is offered but not required. Given Oklahoma's elevated uninsured motorist rate, this coverage is strongly recommended.

Why are Oklahoma auto insurance rates so high?
Oklahoma sits in the heart of Tornado Alley and experiences some of the worst severe weather in the country — hail, tornadoes, and wind events drive comprehensive and property damage claims year-round. Oklahoma City and Tulsa metros have seen significant rate increases since 2020 driven by catastrophic weather losses. Oklahoma also has an elevated uninsured motorist rate (~13%).

Is comprehensive coverage especially important in Oklahoma?
Yes — Oklahoma's hail and tornado risk makes comprehensive coverage particularly valuable. If you drive a vehicle worth more than $6,000-8,000, comprehensive is almost always worth carrying in Oklahoma given the high probability of hail damage. Review your comprehensive deductible as well — a $1,000 deductible makes sense for minor hail events, but ensure you can afford it before a storm season.

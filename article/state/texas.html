Texas auto insurance: the 5-year rate surge no one talks about.

Texas had the largest auto insurance rate increase of any state over the past five years — up over 60% since 2020. If you haven't shopped since before the pandemic, you're almost certainly overpaying.

BoringRate Editorial  ·  May 2026

No state has seen a steeper climb in auto insurance costs over the past five years than Texas. Rates are up more than 60% since 2020 — the largest increase of any state in the country. The Texas average now sits at roughly $3,136 per year for full coverage.

The causes are well-documented: Texas is catastrophe-exposed on a massive scale. Hailstorms, flooding, and winter weather events have driven claims costs through the roof. The February 2021 winter storm alone generated billions in insurance losses. Add in rapid population growth across the Dallas, Houston, Austin, and San Antonio metros, and you have more cars on the road, more accidents, and higher repair costs hitting simultaneously.

What to know about shopping in Texas

Texas is a competitive market — all major national carriers write business here, and the rate spread between them can be substantial. Progressive and GEICO have historically priced aggressively in Texas. State Farm and Allstate tend to run higher. But the ranking shifts depending on your profile, vehicle, and ZIP code.

One Texas-specific factor: hail coverage. If you drive a newer vehicle, confirm your comprehensive coverage includes hail damage and understand your deductible. Texas ZIP codes in Hail Alley — a corridor running from the Panhandle through Dallas — see disproportionately high comprehensive claims.

Texas average: $3,136/year full coverage — 25% above the national average. Dallas and Houston metros run significantly higher than the state average.

The bottom line for Texas drivers: if you haven't compared quotes since 2022 or earlier, you're likely on a rate that was set before the worst of the surge. Shopping now means you're comparing carriers on 2026 pricing — which for many will be meaningfully lower than what a mid-pandemic renewal locked in.
